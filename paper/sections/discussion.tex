\section{Discussion}

\subsection{CPU Pipeline Limitations}

The CPU-based pipeline demonstrates strong efficiency for moderate input resolutions but becomes increasingly limited as image resolution grows. Since feature detection and descriptor extraction scale with the number of image pixels, higher resolutions introduce substantial computational overhead even when SIMD vectorization is utilized. Additionally, lowering the camera resolution to improve runtime performance introduces challenges in feature matching, particularly for objects observed at larger distances. Reduced spatial detail results in weaker descriptors and decreased robustness under multiscale matching scenarios.

\subsection{GPU Pipeline Trade-offs}

The GPU-accelerated pipeline alleviates many of the computational bottlenecks associated with feature extraction and descriptor computation. However, GPU performance remains dependent on memory transfer costs and overall GPU availability. Although storage buffers significantly reduce data transfer overhead compared to transferring full image buffers, the cost of transferring match data still scales with the number of correspondences retrieved.

Furthermore, GPU performance may vary depending on system conditions. On mobile platforms, the GPU is often shared with other rendering or application processes, which can introduce scheduling variability and reduce the effective computational resources available for feature processing.

\subsection{Geometric Tracking Advantages}

The proposed $\mathrm{SE}(3)$ render-and-track formulation addresses several limitations of purely algebraic pipelines. By incorporating explicit geometric constraints derived from the object model, the system maintains stable pose estimates even under partial visibility and large viewpoint changes. The availability of object-space coordinates during rendering enables direct 2D--3D correspondence lifting, reducing the sensitivity of pose estimation to clustered feature distributions that commonly destabilize homography-based tracking.

\subsection{Comparison with ARCore}

ARCore provides a highly optimized mobile tracking framework with built-in image tracking capabilities. While ARCore achieves strong performance for marker-based tracking scenarios, it operates as a closed system with limited control over the internal tracking pipeline. In practice, ARCore's tracking performance may degrade under strong viewpoint changes or partial occlusions depending on the visual properties of the tracked image.

In contrast, the proposed system provides explicit control over the tracking pipeline and allows the integration of geometry-aware constraints through render-and-track optimization. This flexibility enables improved stability in scenarios where purely appearance-based tracking methods struggle.

\subsection{Future Work}

While the proposed framework focuses on feature-based tracking using keypoint correspondences, an interesting direction for future work is the integration of silhouette-based tracking methods. Keypoint-based pipelines rely heavily on the availability of distinctive image features, which may become sparse under low-texture conditions, motion blur, or significant scale variations. Silhouette-based tracking, which relies on aligning object contours or projected model boundaries with observed image edges, can provide complementary geometric constraints that are less dependent on local texture.

In particular, combining the current feature-based pipeline with silhouette alignment could improve robustness in scenarios where keypoint detection becomes unreliable. Such an approach would leverage the known object geometry to match projected object boundaries against image gradients, potentially providing stronger pose constraints under partial visibility or low-texture surfaces. Investigating the integration of silhouette-based alignment within the proposed render-and-track framework remains an important direction for future research.
