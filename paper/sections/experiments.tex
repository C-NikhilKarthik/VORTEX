\section{Experimental Setup}

To evaluate the proposed tracking framework, we conducted experiments on Android-based mobile devices using a native application pipeline. The experiments compare three system configurations: (i) a CPU-based algebraic tracking pipeline, (ii) a GPU-accelerated feature processing pipeline, and (iii) the proposed geometry-aware $\mathrm{SE}(3)$ render-and-track pipeline.

All experiments were performed using real-time camera input under varying conditions of viewpoint, scale, and partial visibility. The goal of the evaluation is to analyze both computational performance and tracking robustness under realistic edge-device constraints.

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}

The evaluation focuses on the following metrics:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Runtime performance:} Average processing time per frame and achieved frames per second (FPS).
    \item \textbf{Tracking stability:} Visual stability of the estimated pose under viewpoint changes and partial visibility.
    \item \textbf{Tracking accuracy:} Consistency of the estimated pose across consecutive frames.
    \item \textbf{System footprint:} Computational load and memory transfer overhead between CPU and GPU.
\end{itemize}

As shown, the GPU implementation provides substantial acceleration for dense operations. However, to maintain high overall throughput, the pipeline architecture must successfully amortize the $1.7$\,ms total round-trip memory transfer overhead introduced by the PCIe bus.
 
To further characterize the pipeline under real device conditions, Table~\ref{tab:android_profiling} presents measured per-stage timings collected directly from the Android tracking application across 345 frames. Frames are stratified into two operating modes: the \emph{detection phase}, in which the system searches for the target marker (93 frames, mean $\approx$\,227 keypoints), and the \emph{tracked phase}, in which stable pose estimation is maintained (252 frames, mean $\approx$\,409 keypoints).

The measured data reveals that Non-Maximum Suppression is the dominant bottleneck in the GPU pipeline, accounting for $56.1$\,\% of total measured processing time. This cost scales with keypoint count: across the 345 profiled frames, NMS averaged $7.58$\,ms during the detection phase ($\approx$227 keypoints) and $14.19$\,ms during the tracked phase ($\approx$409 keypoints). Per-pixel stages such as Grayscale Conversion ($1.40$\,ms) and frame upload ($0.14$\,ms) remain stable across both modes, confirming that the GPU excels at data-parallel operations but that the variable-length keypoint list processed by NMS and descriptor extraction introduces frame-rate variability.

\subsection{Baseline Systems and Ablation Study}

The proposed pipelines are compared against two baselines: the purely CPU-based algebraic pipeline (using homography estimation) and ARCore's native image tracking capability, which provides marker-based pose estimation within the Android AR ecosystem.

To better understand the contribution of individual components, we perform an ablation study evaluating several variants of our pipeline. Table~\ref{tab:ablation} quantifies the impact of geometric refinement and computational optimizations on both runtime performance and tracking stability.
