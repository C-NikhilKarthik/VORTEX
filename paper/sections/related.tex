\section{Related Work}

\paragraph{Visual SLAM and Dense Pose Estimation.}
Visual SLAM systems estimate camera motion while simultaneously reconstructing scene geometry through feature-based or dense optimization pipelines. These approaches typically rely on persistent mapping, global bundle adjustment, and long-term state estimation. While highly accurate, the computational and memory requirements of such pipelines are substantial, particularly for dense direct formulations that operate on per-pixel photometric alignment. Consequently, deploying full SLAM systems on mobile or embedded devices often introduces significant latency and power consumption, making them unsuitable for many real-time augmented reality scenarios where only object-level pose tracking is required.

\paragraph{Model-Based Tracking for Textureless Objects.}
Model-based approaches explicitly utilize known object geometry to estimate pose by aligning projected model contours with image measurements. A representative example is the edge-based tracking framework proposed by Choi and Christensen, which estimates the 3D pose of textureless objects by aligning projected edges with image gradients within a particle filtering framework \cite{choi2012textureless}. Such methods exploit geometric structure and can track objects lacking strong visual features. However, these approaches typically rely on iterative optimization over dense edge measurements and multiple pose hypotheses, which introduces considerable computational overhead and limits their practicality for real-time mobile deployment. \paragraph{Feature-Based Object Tracking.}
Classical feature-based pipelines estimate object motion by detecting keypoints, computing local descriptors, and matching them across frames. Efficient binary descriptors such as ORB and BRISK enable fast Hamming-distance matching and are therefore widely used in real-time vision systems. Pose is commonly recovered through algebraic formulations such as planar homography estimation or Perspective-$n$-Point (PnP) solvers. While computationally lightweight, these approaches rely purely on image-space correspondences and lack explicit geometric constraints. As a result, they often suffer from instability under large viewpoint changes, rapid scale variations, or planar degeneracies.

\paragraph{Learning-Based Pose Estimation.}
Recent research has explored deep learning models that regress object pose directly from RGB images using convolutional neural networks trained on large datasets. These methods can achieve strong robustness to appearance variations, occlusion, and illumination changes. However, the training process typically requires large annotated datasets and high computational resources. In addition, inference often relies on GPU acceleration, and adapting the model to new objects generally requires retraining or fine-tuning. These constraints make purely learning-based solutions difficult to deploy in many mobile or embedded environments with limited compute budgets.

\paragraph{Render-and-Compare Pose Tracking.}
Another line of work performs pose estimation by rendering synthetic views of known object geometry and comparing them with the observed image. These render-and-compare methods incorporate explicit geometric reasoning and can achieve stable pose estimation by minimizing photometric or geometric alignment errors between rendered and observed images. However, dense render-and-optimize strategies often involve iterative optimization over high-dimensional image representations, making them computationally expensive and challenging to deploy efficiently on resource-constrained hardware.

\paragraph{Hybrid Tracking Frameworks.}
To balance robustness and computational efficiency, several systems combine global feature matching with lightweight local tracking methods such as Lucas--Kanade optical flow. These hybrid approaches allow reliable relocalization while maintaining efficient frame-to-frame tracking. Building upon this principle, our work systematically explores three progressively more capable tracking configurations designed for edge devices: (i) a fully optimized CPU-based algebraic pipeline that exploits SIMD vectorization for efficient feature processing, (ii) a GPU-accelerated feature extraction and matching pipeline implemented using programmable shaders, and (iii) a geometry-aware $\mathrm{SE}(3)$ render-and-track framework that incorporates synthetic reference rendering and explicit 2D--3D correspondence lifting for improved pose stability.
